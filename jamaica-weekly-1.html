⚡ Energy Crisis

Island Goes Dark: The Blackout, the Water Crisis — and the Questions That Remain

A lightning-triggered grid collapse on June 5th cascaded into a water emergency — and a political reckoning over Jamaica's single point of failure.

Jamaica's entire electricity grid went dark at approximately 9 p.m. on Friday, June 5th. The Jamaica Public Service Company (JPS) — the island's sole electricity provider — confirmed a complete system failure affecting all 690,000 customers across every parish. The company said lightning near a major Corporate Area substation simultaneously knocked out five transmission lines, triggering an unprecedented "cascading" shutdown of generation capacity island-wide.

JPS CEO Hugh Grant acknowledged at a Saturday press conference that the cascade was not something the company had anticipated or prepared for. Power was restored to roughly 550,000 customers — about 80 percent — by 6 a.m. Saturday, with full restoration confirmed at 6:30 a.m.

"We cannot continue to hold this nation hostage to a single point of failure." — Opposition Spokesman Ian Hayles, calling for NWC to move critical infrastructure to solar

But the knock-on effect on the National Water Commission (NWC) left approximately 65,000 water customers still without supply into Saturday. Opposition Water Spokesman Ian Hayles attacked the Government's failure to place NWC plants on solar backup, noting the same vulnerability was exposed during Hurricane Melissa. He is demanding a comprehensive energy resilience plan for the water sector within the current parliamentary session.

Energy Minister Daryl Vaz called the outage "unacceptable." Prime Minister Holness echoed that language. The Office of Utilities Regulation (OUR) requested a preliminary JPS report by Monday, June 8th. The Opposition also called for answers, with Spokesman Phillip Paulwell demanding full public accountability from government, JPS and the OUR.

Regulator Reveals $500m Was Sent to Government — Leaving FSC in the Red

The Financial Services Commission's executive director has disclosed a half-billion-dollar transfer to the Government that has pushed the watchdog deep into deficit.

FSC Executive Director Lt Col Keron Burrell disclosed to the Senate's Regulations Committee this week that $500 million was transferred from the regulator's reserves to the Government under a directive from the Ministry of Finance. The revelation came as lawmakers pressed the FSC to explain why it had swung from recording surpluses to posting losses exceeding $500 million.

Burrell identified the transfer as one of three blows to the FSC's finances — alongside rising staff costs from public sector pay reforms and spending triggered by a major 2023 cyberattack. The FSC had projected a $165.67 million surplus for its March 2025 financial year but instead recorded a $502.4 million deficit. The watchdog, which regulates securities, insurance and pensions, is now seeking parliamentary approval to raise its fees to claw back ground.

"What it did was it reduced the reserves that we have. In case we're making losses, the reserves are there to allow us to be a going concern for a longer period of time." — FSC Executive Director Lt Col Keron Burrell

Opposition Finance Spokesperson Julian Robinson has demanded a full government explanation for the withdrawal.

Video Crushes False Claims: Jamaicans ARE Welcome at Blue Lagoon

A newly circulated social media video showing locals freely enjoying Portland's world-famous attraction has directly challenged a long-running narrative of exclusion.

A long-running claim that Jamaican nationals are denied access to the iconic Blue Lagoon in Portland was publicly challenged this week after a video showed local residents enjoying the attraction without restriction. The Jamaica Observer reported on June 8th that the footage, whose narrator appeared sympathetic to beach-access advocates, paradoxically undermined the very narrative it set out to support.

The Blue Lagoon, renowned for the extraordinary mix of cold freshwater springs and Caribbean seawater that produces its signature deep-blue colour, had also been closed for a rehabilitation period earlier in 2026, with a reopening around June 1st anticipated. The new footage confirms it is once again open and welcoming visitors.

Agriculture

Portland Bee Farmers Brace for Worst Season in Years

Honey producers in Portland are facing a deeply difficult season after a failed April honey flow left farmers seven weeks behind schedule. Portland Beekeepers' Association chair Courtney Walker told the Jamaica Observer there is currently "no honey at all" in the parish. Farmers are attempting their first reap of the year in June with low expectations — and with hurricane season now under way, even a tropical depression could prove catastrophic for what remains of the harvest.
